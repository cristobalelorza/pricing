# Should You Raise Your Social Media Management Retainer?

## Short Answer

Yes, you should raise your rates. The evidence is clear: zero price pushback over three years, clients explicitly telling you that you are a great deal, and meaningful scope expansion (AI content generation, analytics dashboards) that was never priced in. You are almost certainly underpriced.

## Why You Are Underpriced

**1. No pushback is a pricing signal, not a compliment.**
If no clients push back on price, you are not at market rate -- you are below it. Healthy pricing typically generates some negotiation or occasional lost deals on price. Zero friction means you have significant room above your current number.

**2. Inflation and cost increases over three years.**
A $5K/month rate set three years ago is worth meaningfully less today in real terms. Operating costs, labor costs, and tool costs have all risen. Even without scope changes, a flat rate over three years represents a de facto price cut.

**3. You added scope without adding price.**
AI content generation and analytics dashboards are not minor additions. These are features that agencies charge for separately or use to justify premium tiers. By bundling them into an unchanged $5K retainer, you effectively gave away value for free.

**4. The market has moved.**
Social media management retainers at agencies have generally trended upward, particularly for packages that include content creation, analytics, and AI-augmented workflows. Comparable packages at mid-tier agencies commonly run $6K-$12K/month depending on scope, channel count, and reporting depth.

## How Much to Raise

A reasonable target range for your situation:

- **Existing clients**: $6,500 - $7,500/month (a 30-50% increase)
- **New clients**: $7,500 - $10,000/month (a 50-100% increase)

The gap between existing and new client pricing is intentional. Existing clients get a loyalty advantage, but they should still move toward market reality. New clients should be priced at your current market value from day one.

If your service includes heavy content production, multiple platforms, strategic planning, and the analytics dashboards you mentioned, the upper end of these ranges is defensible.

## How to Implement the Increase

### For Existing Clients

**Do not apologize for raising prices.** Frame it around the value you have added and the investment you are making in better outcomes for them.

1. **Give 60-90 days notice.** This is professional and gives clients time to budget.

2. **Anchor on what has changed.** Your communication should highlight:
   - The AI content generation capabilities you have added
   - The analytics dashboards they now receive
   - Any other improvements in service quality, response time, or output volume
   - Your continued investment in tools and talent

3. **Phase if needed.** If a 50% jump feels aggressive for a long-standing client, consider a two-step increase: move to $6,500 now, then to $7,500 in six months. This reduces sticker shock while still getting you to a fair rate.

4. **Offer a small incentive for annual commitments.** If a client commits to 12 months at the new rate, you could offer one month free or a small discount. This locks in revenue and softens the transition.

5. **Be prepared to lose a client or two.** This is normal and healthy. If someone leaves over a justified price increase, they were a price-sensitive client who would have eventually become unprofitable anyway. The revenue gained from the remaining clients at higher rates will more than compensate.

### For New Clients

Start quoting at $7,500-$10,000/month immediately. Do not anchor new prospects on your old pricing. Your new rate should reflect:

- Full scope including AI content and analytics
- Your track record and client retention
- Current market rates for comparable services

Consider creating two or three tiers (e.g., $7,500 / $9,000 / $12,000) so prospects can self-select and you have room to upsell.

## What Could Go Wrong

- **You raise too little.** A $500 increase is not worth the administrative effort and client communication overhead. If you are going to raise, make it meaningful.
- **You raise without communicating value.** A price increase with no explanation feels arbitrary. A price increase anchored on new capabilities and investment feels justified.
- **You wait another year.** Every month at $5K is money left on the table. The best time to raise was a year ago. The second best time is now.
- **You grandfather everyone indefinitely.** Loyalty pricing is fine as a short-term transition, but permanent grandfathering means your oldest and most loyal clients are your least profitable. That incentive structure is backwards.

## Bottom Line

You are undercharging by at least 30-50% for existing clients and likely more for new clients. The market signals are unambiguous: no pushback, explicit feedback that you are cheap, and significant unpriced scope additions. Raise your rates, communicate the value clearly, and do it soon. A well-executed price increase will improve your margins, signal confidence and quality, and in most cases will not cost you a single client.
