## Value-Based and Outcomes-Based Pricing

**Price is a measure of value, not a number.**
"When we think about price, we think about it as a measure. Like liter is a measure of volume, price is a measure of value." Use WTP as a proxy for whether buyers truly want what you offer. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Price from value, not from costs.**
"I always believe that pricing should come from value and not from costs. The cost is our problem." Technology costs trend downward; price based on the outcome delivered, not current costs. (Eoghan McCabe)

**Outcomes-based pricing is the future.**
"The whole market is going to go towards outcomes-based pricing. It's just so obviously the correct way to build and sell software." Charge for business outcomes (resolutions, savings, revenue generated) rather than inputs (seats, hours, tokens). (Bret Taylor)

**For B2B services**: Value-based pricing works when you can credibly estimate the ROI of your work. If a security audit prevents a breach worth $2M, pricing the audit at $60K (3% of value) is defensible. Cost-plus ($40K) would leave money on the table. Market rate ($50K) ignores the specific value to this client.

## Willingness to Pay as a Product Tool

**Price before product.**
"The folks at First Round summarized this in four words: price before product, period." Have pricing conversations before you finalize scope. It validates demand and reduces risk. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**WTP informs the roadmap.**
"You cannot prioritize a product roadmap without having a willingness to pay conversation." Focus on the 20% of features or deliverables that drive 80% of WTP. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Pricing is a product function.**
"If we truly believe that we need to build products that customers need, love, value, and are willing to pay for, it is a product function." Pricing should sit close to the people designing the offering, not in finance. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Value creation before value capture.**
"We are more concerned with value creation than value capture." Price to capture a fraction of total value created, ensuring long-term adoption. Have WTP conversations before building, not just when selling. (Julia Schottenstein)

**For B2B services**: Before scoping a proposal, ask the client what budget they have set aside, what the problem costs them today, and what solving it would be worth. This is not negotiation -- it is research that informs your pricing.

## Pricing Models and Structures

**How you charge matters more than how much.**
"How you charge is way more important than how much you charge." The pricing model (unit of value) often dictates success more than the specific price point. Choose between subscription, usage-based, project-based, or hybrid models based on how buyers perceive value. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Three core strategies.**
There are only three pricing strategies: Skimming (premium, signal quality), Penetration (volume, capture market share), and Maximization (balance price and volume). Pick one and execute consistently. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Keep pricing simple.**
"In your early days it is by far more important to have pricing that is really simple and it's not creating too much friction." Test whether buyers can articulate your pricing back to you. If they cannot, it is too complex. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Simplicity over perfect value capture.**
"The biggest mistake was a lot of mistakes compounded." Resist the urge to add endless tiers and add-ons. A simple, understandable billing structure beats a complex value-alignment model. (Paul Adams)

**Hybrid models work best for most.**
"The vast majority have a hybrid approach -- some good, better, best subscription model." Combine fixed tiers with usage-based limits for predictability and growth. Use quota limits as natural upgrade triggers. (Naomi Ionita)

**For B2B services**: The simplest structure that fits the engagement is usually the right one. A $50K project fee is easier to sell, deliver, and invoice than a complex hybrid of hourly rates, milestone payments, and performance bonuses.

## Freemium, Trials, and Entry Points

**Reduce early friction to extend the value-discovery runway.**
"When you can lower the barriers to monetary friction, you can actually causally change your ability to become successful." Lowering the initial cost extends the buyer's time to discover value. (Archie Abrams)

**Sample premium value, don't hide it.**
"What if we actually sampled a number of different paid suggestions and interspersed them to free users?" Show buyers what the premium experience looks like rather than hiding it behind a hard paywall. (Albert Cheng)

**Free-to-paid requires awareness.**
"The majority of free users are often unaware of what is actually included in paid plans." Track pricing page views per activated account. Ensure free users see paid triggers at least three times. (Elena Verna)

**Reverse trial combines the best of both.**
Offer a time-limited trial of premium features to all new signups, then maintain a free-forever tier. Users experience full value first, then settle into the tier that fits. (Lauryn Isford)

**The aha moment must be free.**
"If it gets you to the aha moment, that path to habit formation, that has to be free." Keep core utility in the free tier to collapse time to value. (Naomi Ionita)

**Day 1 vs Day 100 features.**
Distinguish between features that provide immediate value (Day 1) and those that provide value after significant usage (Day 100). Keep the initial paid plan simple by focusing on Day 1 premium value. Push Day 100 features to higher tiers. (Naomi Ionita)

**For B2B services**: The equivalent of a free trial is a paid discovery workshop or pilot engagement. Price it at or near cost. It lets the buyer experience your work quality and builds momentum toward the full engagement.

## Enterprise and Sales-Led Pricing

**The self-serve ceiling.**
"Self-serve monetization has a cap of about $10,000. That's just how much we're able to process on credit cards before they start getting flagged." Above $10K-$15K, you need a sales-led motion. (Elena Verna)

**Enterprise ACV anchoring.**
"Enterprise companies are very used to an initial contract somewhere between $75K and $150K." Avoid selling small-business pricing to enterprise buyers -- it anchors too low and makes expansion difficult. (Jen Abel)

**Don't discount to close.**
"Aggressive discounting is a sign of poor qualification. High-value clients who are truly bought in will respect the price." Use price resistance as a qualification signal. (Jen Abel)

**No hesitation means underpriced.**
"In that moment, the exec said 'Okay, sure. Sounds good.' Not a second of hesitation." If buyers consistently accept without negotiation, you are significantly underpriced. Test the upper bounds. Accept losing 20-30% of deals on price as a sign you are near the market ceiling. (Naomi Ionita)

**For B2B services**: When an enterprise prospect accepts your first proposal without pushback, your next proposal to a similar client should be 20-30% higher. Repeat until you find resistance.

## Price Increases and Iteration

**Never set and forget.**
"Do not set it and forget it. I see companies do this, where they labor over designs and features..." Treat pricing as a living part of the business. Revisit every 6-12 months. (Naomi Ionita)

**Most pricing changes are reversible.**
"Move faster on reversible decisions like pricing by grandfathering existing users." Grandfather existing clients on old pricing. New pricing applies to new clients. This lets you move fast with low risk. (Eeke de Milliano)

**Pricing power is the health test.**
"The true definition of a company is pricing power. And if you have a prayer session before doing a 10% price increase..." If raising prices feels terrifying, your value proposition may be weak. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Three common pricing mistakes.**
The three most common mistakes: (1) waiting too long to monetize, (2) underpricing through lack of segmentation, (3) failing to iterate as the offering evolves. (Naomi Ionita)

**De-feature rather than discount.**
"Before you price discount, think about what value can you exchange to justify that discount." In a downturn, create a lesser tier by removing features rather than simply cutting the price. Preserve price integrity. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**For B2B services**: Annual rate increases of 3-8% are standard and expected. Larger increases should be tied to a clear value story -- new capabilities, stronger team, better track record, expanded scope.

## Pricing Psychology and Behavioral Economics

**Use behavioral pricing thoughtfully.**
"Behavioral pricing basically is tapping into the irrational modes of our decision making." Account for psychological biases: decoys make the middle option attractive, psychological thresholds matter ($99 to $101 feels bigger than $97 to $99), three tiers nudge toward the middle. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Discounting signals value.**
"What discounting has a really powerful effect on is it can signal value with a high list price, but then bring something down to an affordable price." Use high list prices to establish a quality anchor, then use targeted discounts to drive action. (Archie Abrams)

**Pricing signals quality.**
"The discounting model makes sense when something's commoditized. But chasms are based on risk-bearing decisions." When selling something new or unfamiliar, discounting signals low confidence. Use value-based pricing to signal commitment. (Geoffrey Moore)

**For B2B services**: In proposals, present the stretch price first, then the target, then the floor. The stretch anchors the buyer's perception. The target then feels reasonable. The floor exists as a safety net but should feel like a stripped-down option.

## Marketplace Pricing

**Commissions fund demand-side benefits.**
"Your sensitivity to that commission is much harder to understand." Higher commissions are justifiable when they fund benefits that attract demand for your supply side. (Dan Hockenmaier)

**Move deeper to justify higher take rates.**
"Marketplaces can justify higher commissions by moving deeper into the value chain and taking on more operational responsibility." If you handle logistics, trust, risk, or quality assurance, you earn a higher commission. (Dan Hockenmaier)

**Align monetization with value delivery.**
"The main thing we did is to rebuild that whole loop, change the monetization model." Shift charging events to moments of high intent or value delivery, not arbitrary touchpoints. (Noam Lovinsky)

## AI and Automation Pricing

**Outcomes over tokens.**
"There's a huge difference between outcomes-based pricing and usage-based pricing because especially in AI, they're not necessarily even correlated." Usage metrics like tokens are poor proxies for value. Charge for outcomes. (Bret Taylor)

**Don't anchor AI pricing too low.**
"The winners in AI will need to master monetization from day one. If you start training your customers to expect $20 a month..." Identify the 20% of AI capabilities that drive 80% of WTP. Avoid anchoring at low price points. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Avoid all-you-can-eat for high-cost AI.**
"People are willing to pay more." AI products should move away from flat pricing toward usage-based or outcomes-based models that reflect both cost and value. (Eric Simons)

**Treat free AI usage as marketing spend.**
"You have to remove the barrier of entry. You have to give a lot of your product away for free." In new AI categories, treat LLM pass-through costs for free users as a marketing expense, not COGS. (Elena Verna)

**For B2B services using AI**: If your service uses AI to deliver faster or better results, price based on the outcome (time saved, quality delivered), not the AI cost. The buyer does not care about your inference costs -- they care about what you delivered.

## Packaging and Bundling

**Leaders, Fillers, Killers.**
"The quick framework to think about packaging: leaders, fillers, and killers." Leaders are deliverables >50% of buyers want and will pay for. Fillers add perceived volume. Killers are niche items that only 10-20% want -- sell them as add-ons, because including them devalues the bundle. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Segmentation by needs, not demographics.**
"Segmentation needs to be based on what customers need, what they value, and what they are willing to pay for." Build different versions for different needs. Avoid one-size-fits-all. (Madhavan Ramanujam)

**Gate growth loops, not value.**
"Initially the way that it worked was our starter team was that you could have unlimited files but only collaborate with two or three people." Gate on volume (number of projects, records, hours) rather than network effects (number of collaborators, viewers). (Claire Butler)

**Charge makers, not viewers.**
"We decided that we're only going to charge for people when they make a document. I wanted no friction on the share edge." Remove financial friction from the primary viral action. (Shishir Mehrotra)

**Land with one product, cross-sell later.**
"Bundling is not a great land strategy for PLG companies." Land with a single, clear offering. Use bundles for upsell and expansion, not initial sale. (Carilu Dietrich)

**For B2B services**: Package core deliverables as the base engagement. Sell training, documentation, extended support, and maintenance as add-ons. Do not bundle everything into one price -- it makes it impossible for the buyer to see where the value is.

## Common Mistakes

**Gut-feel pricing.**
Setting prices based on intuition rather than WTP research, cost analysis, or market benchmarks. Even informal buyer conversations are better than nothing.

**Waiting too long to monetize.**
"Another regret is I would've tried to monetize and generate revenue sooner." Early monetization validates the business model and provides operational flexibility. (Ryan Hoover)

**Underpricing through lack of segmentation.**
One price for all buyers means you are either leaving money on the table with high-value buyers or pricing out low-value ones. Segment and price accordingly. (Naomi Ionita)

**Complex pricing that breaks self-serve.**
"Your pricing needs to be relatively simple. If your pricing is super complicated, they need to..." Complex pricing requires sales assistance, which breaks product-led and self-serve motions. (Hila Qu)

**Competing on price rather than value.**
"Discounting is ineffective when crossing the chasm because the buyer's primary concern is risk, not price." Focus on demonstrating value and reducing risk rather than cutting price. (Geoffrey Moore)

**Ignoring the self-serve ceiling.**
Attempting $50K deals via credit card checkout. Above $10K-$15K, build a sales motion. (Elena Verna)

**Treating pricing as finance's job.**
Pricing should inform product design and value creation. It belongs close to the people building the offering, not in a spreadsheet in the finance department. (Madhavan Ramanujam)
