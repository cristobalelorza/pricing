## Good-Better-Best for Services

The three-tier model works for professional services just as it does for SaaS. The key is making the tiers feel like genuinely different levels of engagement, not arbitrary feature gates.

### Tier Design

**Good (Entry tier)**
- Purpose: remove barriers to entry, let buyers test the relationship
- Includes: core deliverables, standard timelines, email support
- Price: accessible, low commitment
- Target: budget-conscious buyers, first-time engagements, small scope

**Better (Recommended tier)**
- Purpose: where most buyers should land
- Includes: full deliverables, reasonable scope, priority support, some customization
- Price: your anchor price -- the one you optimize around
- Target: serious buyers, growing companies, repeatable engagements

**Best (Premium tier)**
- Purpose: capture maximum value from high-value buyers
- Includes: everything, plus advanced deliverables, dedicated resources, SLA, strategic access
- Price: 2-3x the Better tier
- Target: enterprise, complex engagements, buyers who value speed or certainty

### Example: Marketing Agency Tiers

```
                  Starter           Growth            Enterprise
                  $3,000/mo         $8,000/mo         $20,000/mo

Strategy          Quarterly review  Monthly strategy   Dedicated strategist
Content           4 posts/mo        12 posts/mo        Unlimited
Channels          2 platforms       4 platforms        All platforms
Reporting         Monthly report    Weekly dashboard   Real-time + custom
Support           Email             Priority + Slack   Dedicated team
Onboarding        Self-guided       Guided setup       White-glove
```

### Example: Consulting Engagement Tiers

```
                  Assessment        Implementation     Transformation
                  $15,000           $45,000            $120,000

Scope             Diagnosis only    Fix identified      End-to-end
```

## Tier Differentiation Strategies

How to create meaningful separation between tiers:

**Scope gating**: Same quality of work, different breadth
- Basic: audit of 1 system; Pro: audit of all systems
- Works when value scales linearly with scope

**Deliverable depth**: Same scope, different thoroughness
- Basic: findings report; Pro: findings + implementation plan + training
- Works when buyers have different needs for the same problem

**Access and speed**: Same work, different responsiveness
- Standard: 5-day turnaround; Priority: 48-hour turnaround
- Works when time-to-value matters and some buyers will pay for speed

**Support level**: Same deliverables, different ongoing relationship
- Email support vs priority Slack vs dedicated account manager
- Works for ongoing engagements where support burden varies

**Customization**: Standard vs tailored
- Template-based vs fully custom
- Works when some buyers need bespoke solutions and others are fine with standard

## Packaging for Buyer Personas

### Identifying Pricing Personas

Different buyers have different willingness to pay, feature needs, and buying processes. Segment by:

- **Company size**: solopreneur, SMB (2-50), mid-market (50-500), enterprise (500+)
- **Use case**: different problems have different value
- **Sophistication**: beginner buyers need hand-holding; experienced buyers want efficiency
- **Industry**: different sectors have different budget norms and buying cycles

### Persona-Based Packaging

**Step 1: Define personas**

| Persona | Size | Primary need | WTP | Buying process |
|---------|------|-------------|-----|----------------|
| Freelancer | 1 person | Get it done cheaply | Low | Credit card, instant |
| Small team | 2-20 | Reliable partner | Medium | 1-2 decision makers |
| Growing company | 20-200 | Scale + expertise | Higher | Committee, 2-4 weeks |
| Enterprise | 200+ | Risk reduction + compliance | High | Procurement, 2-6 months |

**Step 2: Map deliverables to personas**

| Deliverable | Freelancer | Small team | Growing | Enterprise |
|-------------|-----------|------------|---------|------------|
| Core service | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Dedicated PM | -- | -- | Yes | Yes |
| Custom reporting | -- | -- | Yes | Yes |
| SLA guarantee | -- | -- | -- | Yes |
| Security review | -- | -- | -- | Yes |
| Exec sponsor access | -- | -- | -- | Yes |

**Step 3: Price to value for each persona**
- Research WTP per segment
- Set prices that capture value without blocking adoption at entry level
- Consider segment-specific proposals or landing pages

## Freemium vs Free Trial vs Pilot

### When to Offer a Free Tier (Freemium)

Works when:
- The offering has network effects or viral potential
- Free users provide value (referrals, content, data)
- Large market where percentage conversion drives volume
- Low marginal cost to serve free users

Risks:
- Free users may never convert
- Devalues the offering's perception
- Support costs for non-paying users
- Harder to raise prices later

### When to Offer a Free Trial

Works when:
- The offering needs time to demonstrate value
- Significant onboarding or setup investment required
- B2B with buying committees that need to see results first
- Higher price points where buyers want proof

Best practices:
- 7-14 days for simple offerings, 14-30 for complex ones
- Full access during trial (not feature-limited)
- Clear countdown and follow-up sequence

### When to Offer a Paid Pilot (Best for B2B Services)

Works when:
- The engagement is large and the buyer needs risk reduction
- You need to demonstrate competence before a full contract
- Both sides benefit from a scoped test

Structure:
- 2-4 week pilot with specific deliverables and success criteria
- Price at cost or slim margin (not free -- free devalues the work)
- Clear conversion path: "If the pilot succeeds, here's what the full engagement looks like"

This is often the best approach for B2B services. It reduces buyer risk, demonstrates your capability, and creates momentum toward the full engagement. (Archie Abrams: reducing early friction extends the buyer's runway to discover your value.)

### Hybrid Approaches

**Reverse trial**: Start with full premium access for a limited time, then downgrade to a free or basic tier. The buyer experiences maximum value first. (Lauryn Isford)

**Discovery workshop**: A paid, time-boxed engagement that produces a deliverable (audit, roadmap, assessment) and naturally leads to an implementation engagement. Functions as both a paid pilot and a sales tool.

## Enterprise Pricing

### When to Add "Contact Sales" Pricing

Add custom pricing when:
- Deal sizes exceed $10K-$15K annually (Elena Verna: self-serve caps around $10K due to credit card limits and individual spending authority)
- Buyers need custom contracts, MSAs, or procurement processes
- Implementation or onboarding requires dedicated resources
- Security, compliance, or audit requirements exist
- The engagement scope varies significantly between buyers

### Enterprise Tier Elements

**Table stakes (expected by enterprise buyers):**
- SSO / SAML
- Audit logs and admin controls
- Uptime SLA
- Security certifications (SOC 2, etc.)
- Custom contract terms

**Value-adds (justify premium pricing):**
- Dedicated support or success manager
- Custom onboarding and training
- Priority roadmap input or co-development
- Executive business reviews
- Custom integrations

### Enterprise Pricing Models

**Per-seat at scale**: Volume discounts for large teams
- Example: $175/user/month (standard) to $120/user/month (100+ users)

**Platform fee + usage**: Base fee for access, usage-based above thresholds
- Example: $2,000/month base + $50 per additional project

**Value-based contracts**: Price tied to buyer outcomes
- Example: percentage of cost savings, revenue share, per-resolution fee

**For B2B services**: Enterprise ACV should typically anchor at $75K-$150K for initial contracts. This aligns with corporate buying habits and avoids the trap of anchoring too low, which makes future expansion hard to defend. (Jen Abel)

## Pricing Page and Proposal Best Practices

### For Pricing Pages (SaaS / Productized Services)

**Above the fold:**
- Clear tier comparison table
- Recommended tier highlighted
- Monthly / annual toggle (annual discount of 17-20%)
- Primary CTA per tier

**Common elements:**
- Feature comparison table
- Tier audience labels ("For growing teams", "For enterprises")
- FAQ section addressing common objections
- Annual discount callout
- Money-back guarantee or pilot option
- Client logos and trust signals

### For Service Proposals

- Lead with the business problem, not the price
- Present floor / target / stretch as options, not as negotiation
- Make the target option the obvious best value (decoy effect)
- State assumptions and scope boundaries explicitly
- Include timeline and milestones
- Show the team and their qualifications
- Address risk and how you mitigate it
- End with next steps and a clear call to action

### Pricing Psychology in Practice

- **Anchoring**: Present the highest-priced option first. The target option then feels reasonable by comparison.
- **Decoy effect**: If presenting three options, structure the middle one as the best value. The top tier exists partly to make the middle tier look good.
- **Charm pricing**: $49 vs $50 signals value-consciousness. $50 vs $49 signals premium. Choose based on your positioning.
- **The compromise effect**: When three options are presented, buyers disproportionately choose the middle. This is why Good-Better-Best works.
- **Specificity signals research**: $47,500 feels more carefully calculated than $50,000. But do not manufacture fake precision from vague inputs.
- **Round pricing for premium**: Premium services often benefit from clean round numbers ($100K, not $98,750) because they signal confidence, not bargain-hunting.
